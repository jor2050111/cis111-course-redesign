## Option 1: Theme-Density

### Strategy

Pair modules within their Part to maximize within-week thematic coherence. Pairs are chosen so each paired week reads as a single integrated topic block rather than two separately-themed modules.

### Pairing Decisions

**Pair A: M2 + M3 (Week 2, Part I close)**

* **Rationale:** Both modules examine ethics at the institutional scale. M2 covers the IT worker's six professional relationships; M3 zooms out to corporate governance, CSR, and the AI governance anchor. Together they answer "how does ethical reasoning scale from the individual professional to the organization?"
* **Combined CLOs:** II, III
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~11,500
* **Activity load:** Two Skills Labs (IT Contractor Dilemma + AI Hiring Tool Dilemma), both Apply-level

**Pair B: M5 + M6 (Week 4, Part II close)**

* **Rationale:** Both modules center on stakeholder-protection ethics. M5's whistleblowing and fiduciary duty connect directly to M6's HIPAA-grounded patient privacy. Both ask "what do organizations owe to those whose data they hold, and what happens when an insider sees that duty being violated?"
* **Combined CLOs:** V, VII, VI
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~12,250
* **Activity load:** DataStream Dilemma + Healthcare AI Deployment analysis

**Pair C: M9 + M10 (Week 7, Parts III-IV bridge)**

* **Rationale:** Both modules treat rights-based ethics in digital contexts. M9 covers IP rights and fair use; M10 covers expression rights, defamation, and workplace privacy. Both rest on the same legal-ethical scaffolding (rights, limits, balancing tests) and both have major AI touchpoints (AI-generated works in M9, AI content moderation in M10).
* **Combined CLOs:** XI, XII, XIII
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~12,900
* **Activity load:** IP analysis + Employee Monitoring Policy draft, both Evaluate-level

| 1 | May 26 - 31 | M1 | Foundations: ethical theories | I | Partial week (6 days), term-launch onboarding |
| 2 | Jun 1 - 7 | M2 + M3 | Professional & corporate ethics, AI governance anchor | II, III | Paired week, Part I close |
| 3 | Jun 8 - 14 | M4 | Social media responsibility | IV | Single |
| 4 | Jun 15 - 21 | M5 + M6 | Stakeholder-protection ethics (whistleblowing + healthcare) | V, VII, VI | Paired week, Part II close |

### Strengths

* Strongest narrative arc: each paired week has a clear unifying theme that students can name in one sentence
* Pairs preserve the existing four-Part structure
* Capstone (M12) gets its own clean week at term end

### Risks and Compromises

* No buffer for capstone scaffolding; M12 work has to fit inside W9's 6 instructional days
* M8 sits in W6 (July 4 light week); its post-polish ~6,250 is the heaviest single load and may stretch the reduced-availability week
* M11 is the heaviest single by cognitive load (Create-level, 4 CLOs) and lands in W8 with no companion

### Capstone Foreshadowing Pattern

Module 12 work begins to surface in W7 paired week, where M9+M10's evaluative depth previews the synthesis demanded by the capstone. Students should be told in W5 (M7's cost/benefit analysis Skills Lab) that the capstone will require integrating multiple frameworks; M11's W8 incident-response policy work is the last full-MLO Create-level practice before the capstone itself.

### Pair Justification Test

* **Why M2+M3 and not M1+M2?** M1 is the foundation single; pairing M1 with anything compromises the deliberate Week 1 onboarding posture. M2+M3 keeps Part I flowing without consuming the foundation slot.
* **Why M5+M6 and not M4+M5?** M4 (social media) is platform-economics-driven; M5 and M6 share the deeper structural theme of fiduciary duty and protected populations. M5+M6 reads as one unified ethics block; M4+M5 would read as two unrelated topics.
* **Why M9+M10 and not M10+M11?** M11 is too dense (4 CLOs, Create-level) to share a week with anything. M9+M10 are both rights-based and Evaluate-level — natural pair.

## Option 2: Length-Balance

### Strategy

Pair the lightest post-polish standalone modules with the heaviest post-polish standalone modules so every paired week lands at roughly the same combined load (~12,000 words). Cognitive-load pairing accepts thematic break in exchange for smoother week-to-week pacing.

### Pairing Decisions

**Pair A: M2 + M11 (Week 2, lightest professional + heaviest security)**

* **Rationale:** M2 is one of the two lightest post-polish modules (~5,500); M11 is among the heaviest by CLO count (4 CLOs, Create-level MLO). Pairing them gives Week 2 a balanced ~12,000-word load and front-loads M11 so its dense security-policy work happens while students are still high-energy in early term.
* **Combined CLOs:** II, XIV, XV, XVI, XVIII
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~12,000
* **Activity load:** IT Contractor Dilemma (Apply) + Incident Response Plan (Create)

**Pair B: M3 + M9 (Week 3, mid + upper-band)**

* **Rationale:** M3 (~6,000) and M9 (~6,500) combine to ~12,500. Both touch property and ownership questions (corporate ownership in M3, IP ownership in M9), giving the pair a thin but real thematic thread.
* **Combined CLOs:** III, XI
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~12,500
* **Activity load:** AI Hiring Tool Dilemma (Apply) + IP Analysis (Evaluate)

**Pair C: M4 + M10 (Week 4, paired rights-and-platforms)**

* **Rationale:** M4 (~6,000) + M10 (~6,400) = ~12,400. Both deal with platform-mediated speech and behavior. The pairing lands at target combined load and recovers the rights-based connection that Option 1 placed in W7.
* **Combined CLOs:** IV, XII, XIII
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~12,400
* **Activity load:** Social Media Audit + Employee Monitoring Policy

### Week-by-Week Schedule

| Week | Dates | Module(s) | Theme | CLOs | Notes |
|------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-------|
| 1 | May 26 - 31 | M1 | Foundations: ethical theories | I | Partial week, term-launch onboarding |
| 2 | Jun 1 - 7 | M2 + M11 | Professional ethics + cybersecurity (length-balanced) | II, XIV, XV, XVI, XVIII | Paired week, dense early load |
| 3 | Jun 8 - 14 | M3 + M9 | Corporate governance + IP (ownership thread) | III, XI | Paired week |
| 4 | Jun 15 - 21 | M4 + M10 | Platforms & rights | IV, XII, XIII | Paired week |
| 5 | Jun 22 - 28 | M5 | Whistleblowing & fiduciary duty | V, VII | Single |
| 6 | Jun 29 - Jul 5 | M6 | Healthcare IT ethics | VI | Single, July 4 holiday — mid-weight single |
| 7 | Jul 6 - 12 | M7 | Software liability & secure coding | VIII, IX | Single |
| 8 | Jul 13 - 19 | M8 | Government regulation | X | Single |
| 9 | Jul 20 - 25 | M12 | Capstone | XVII | Partial week, final instructional module |

### Strengths

* Most even weekly load: every paired week ~12,000 words, every standalone week ~6,000-6,500
* All three pairs front-loaded into Weeks 2-4, so the back half of the term has predictable single-module rhythm
* W6 (July 4 light week) holds M6 (mid-weight, ~6,250) — a reasonable holiday-week single

### Risks and Compromises

* Thematic flow breaks twice: M2 (professional ethics) + M11 (cybersecurity) is a hard topic jump in W2; M3 (Part I) + M9 (Part III) compresses two Parts into one week
* Front-loading M11 into W2 means students hit a 4-CLO Create-level module before they have practiced Apply-level reasoning across the course
* The Part I → Part IV → Part III → Part II → Part III topic order is dis-orderly; instructors who teach the course as a narrative arc may find this option pedagogically rough

### Capstone Foreshadowing Pattern

Because M11 is paired into W2, students see a Create-level MLO (formulate security policies) in the first paired week. This previews the capstone's synthesis demand very early. Subsequent Evaluate-level work in M9 (W3), M10 (W4), and M7 (W7) reinforces the pattern. M12 in W9 then asks students to repeat what they have already practiced four times.

### Pair Justification Test

* **Why M2+M11 and not M1+M11?** M1 is reserved for W1 foundation. M2 is the next-lightest module and the first eligible paired slot.
* **Why M3+M9 and not M3+M10?** M9 has a stronger ownership-themed connection to M3 (corporate ownership ↔ IP ownership). M3+M10 has weaker thematic glue.
* **Why M4+M10 and not M4+M11?** M11 is paired with M2 to anchor early-term cognitive density. Pairing M11 again would over-load. M4+M10 gives the term its second rights/platforms week without re-using M11.

## Option 3: Capstone-Buffer

### Strategy

Use four pairs concentrated in Weeks 2-5 so the back half of the term opens up a dedicated capstone-development runway. M12 anchors the final instructional week (W8) and W9 becomes a capstone-focused buffer week with no new module content. Students get an extra week to build, revise, and present capstone work.

### Pairing Decisions

**Pair A: M2 + M3 (Week 2, Part I close)**

* **Rationale:** Identical to Option 1 Pair A. Tightest within-Part pair available.
* **Combined CLOs:** II, III
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~11,500

**Pair B: M4 + M5 (Week 3, Part II open)**

* **Rationale:** Both modules treat platform/organization accountability to outsiders (users in M4, the public in M5). Pair occupies the Part II opening slot at ~12,000 words combined.
* **Combined CLOs:** IV, V, VII
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~12,000

**Pair C: M6 + M7 (Week 4, Part II/III bridge)**

* **Rationale:** Both modules grapple with how IT responsibility translates into harm to people. M6 (healthcare AI accountability) and M7 (software liability and safety-critical systems) share the underlying question "who is responsible when technology hurts someone?"
* **Combined CLOs:** VI, VIII, IX
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~12,500

**Pair D: M8 + M9 (Week 5, Part III close)**

* **Rationale:** Both modules are external-legal-framework modules. M8 covers government/regulatory frameworks; M9 covers IP frameworks. Together they complete Part III.
* **Combined CLOs:** X, XI
* **Combined post-polish words:** ~12,750

### Week-by-Week Schedule

| Week | Dates | Module(s) | Theme | CLOs | Notes |
|------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-------|
| 1 | May 26 - 31 | M1 | Foundations: ethical theories | I | Partial week, term-launch onboarding |
| 2 | Jun 1 - 7 | M2 + M3 | Professional & corporate ethics, AI governance anchor | II, III | Paired week, Part I close |
| 3 | Jun 8 - 14 | M4 + M5 | Platform & whistleblower accountability | IV, V, VII | Paired week, Part II open |
| 4 | Jun 15 - 21 | M6 + M7 | When IT causes harm (healthcare + software) | VI, VIII, IX | Paired week, Part II/III bridge |
| 5 | Jun 22 - 28 | M8 + M9 | External legal frameworks (regulation + IP) | X, XI | Paired week, Part III close |
| 6 | Jun 29 - Jul 5 | M10 | Free expression & workplace privacy | XII, XIII | Single, July 4 holiday — light Evaluate-level single |
| 7 | Jul 6 - 12 | M11 | Cybersecurity & risk | XIV, XV, XVI, XVIII | Single, heaviest module |
| 8 | Jul 13 - 19 | M12 | Capstone instruction begins | XVII | Single, final instructional module |
| 9 | Jul 20 - 25 | (none) | Capstone development buffer | — | Partial week, no new content; capstone build/revise/submit |

### Strengths

* **Strongest capstone preparation of the three options.** W9 is dedicated buffer; M12 work begins in W8 and continues through W9 with full instructor attention
* W6 (July 4 light week) holds M10 — Evaluate-level single, mid-weight, well-suited to reduced student availability
* M11 sits alone in W7 with no competition for student attention — its 4 CLOs and Create-level MLO get full focus

### Risks and Compromises

* Front half of the term is **dense**: four consecutive paired weeks (W2-W5) at ~12,000+ words each. Students who fall behind in the first month may struggle to recover
* M4+M5 spans Part II without strong thematic glue (social media platforms ≠ whistleblower ethics, but both about external accountability)
* M6+M7 crosses the Part II/III boundary — pedagogically defensible but loosens the Part structure
* Total instructional content is delivered in 8 weeks (W1-W8) instead of 9, intensifying the per-week pace

### Capstone Foreshadowing Pattern

Because content concludes in W8, M12 work becomes visible to students starting W6 (in announcements and Skills Lab framing). The capstone scaffold should be released no later than end of W6 so students have W7 (M11 single) to ideate while finishing the security module. W8 introduces capstone formally; W9 is execution. This option produces the longest active capstone-work window of the three.

### Pair Justification Test

* **Why four pairs in Weeks 2-5 and not spread across the term?** Spreading pairs creates a back-loaded term with no buffer; concentration creates the buffer at end of term where capstone work needs it most.
* **Why M4+M5 and not M5+M6 (Option 1's choice)?** M5+M6 is the strongest Part II pair, but using it forces a different Part II/III bridge that doesn't preserve M6's natural connection to M7's harm-and-liability theme. M4+M5 / M6+M7 is the cleanest 4-pair Part II decomposition.
* **Why M10 alone in W6 and not paired?** W6 cannot host a paired week per the addendum (July 4 reduces availability). M10 is the lightest Part IV single and lands well there.

## Recommendation Note

This document presents three options without recommending one. Selection is an instructor decision based on:

* Cohort readiness (Option 2's W2 paired M11 demands strong prior preparation)
* Prior summer-term experience (Option 3's dense W2-W5 has worked for instructors who pace tightly early)
* Capstone scope (Option 3 best supports capstones requiring substantial student-built artifacts; Options 1 and 2 fit narrower-scope capstones)
* Tolerance for thematic fragmentation vs. rigid Part flow (Option 1 strict, Option 3 moderate, Option 2 loose)

Once a selection is made, record it in this file (or a successor) and update each module's Looking Ahead forward reference to match the selected pairing. Until selection occurs, all 12 module Looking Ahead sections remain provisionally PASS for rubric criterion B8.
