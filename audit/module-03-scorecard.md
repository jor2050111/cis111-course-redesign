## Notes

**B2 currency refresh detail.** M3 is the AI ethics anchor for the entire course, so B2 is the highest-stakes judgment criterion here. Pre-polish, the module referenced the EU AI Act and NIST AI RMF correctly but without current specificity (no phase-in dates, no generative AI profile, no state-level law examples, no court accountability cases). The post-polish additions give students a concrete picture of where AI governance stands in 2025-2026: the EU Act is in mid-enforcement, the NIST framework has been extended to generative AI, state laws are proliferating in the US, and courts have begun resolving accountability disputes that the governance frameworks left ambiguous.

**Air Canada and Workday selection rationale.** These two cases were chosen because they sit directly inside the module's domain (corporate governance, accountability for AI outputs) rather than in downstream modules' territory. The Air Canada case is a clean corporate accountability ruling; the Workday case involves the hiring AI scenario that is also the Skills Lab subject, making the real-world case a direct anchor for the Skills Lab analysis. Students completing the RecruitIQ Skills Lab scenario will recognize the Workday parallel, reinforcing why governance gaps matter beyond the hypothetical.

**Orchestrator-verifier placement.** The orchestrator-verifier framing is introduced in M2 at the individual IT worker level (ACM/IEEE professional obligations). In M3, it appears at the organizational governance level (who is accountable when an organization deploys AI). This progression from individual to organizational accountability tracks the module's CLO III focus and sets up M5's whistleblowing context, M6's clinical AI accountability, M7's product liability questions, and M8's regulatory compliance framing. The frame repeats in every module that follows, but M3 is where the organizational governance rationale for it is established.
