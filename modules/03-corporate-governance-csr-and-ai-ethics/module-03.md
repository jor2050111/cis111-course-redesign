## 3.1 Shareholders, Stakeholders, and Corporate Governance

When a company makes an ethical decision, the first question is: whose interests should it prioritize? Two competing theories offer very different answers.

### Shareholder Theory

**Shareholder theory** argues that a corporation's primary obligation is to maximize profits for its owners (shareholders). The economist Milton Friedman made the most famous case for this position in 1970. He argued that the only social responsibility of a business is to increase its profits, as long as it stays within the rules of the game. In this view, executives who spend company money on social causes are essentially spending someone else's money without their permission.

In the technology industry, shareholder theory shows up whenever a company prioritizes revenue growth, stock price, or quarterly earnings above other concerns. When a social media platform delays safety features because they would reduce user engagement, that decision reflects shareholder-first thinking.

### Stakeholder Theory

**Stakeholder theory** takes a broader view. It argues that a corporation has obligations to everyone affected by its operations, not just shareholders. R. Edward Freeman developed this framework in 1984. A **stakeholder** is any person or group that can affect, or is affected by, a company's actions.

For a technology company, stakeholders typically include:

* **Shareholders and investors** who provide capital and expect returns
* **Employees** who depend on the company for income and working conditions
* **Customers and users** who rely on the company's products and services
* **Communities** affected by the company's operations, hiring, and environmental impact
* **Suppliers and contractors** including gig workers and outsourced labor
* **Government and regulators** who set the legal framework for business conduct

Stakeholder theory says that long-term business success depends on maintaining healthy relationships with all of these groups, not just maximizing short-term returns for one of them.

### Corporate Governance: Who Decides?

**Corporate governance** is the system of rules, practices, and processes by which a company is directed and controlled. At most large companies, the key players include:

* **The board of directors**, which oversees the company's strategy and holds executive leadership accountable
* **Executive leadership** (CEO, CTO, CFO), which makes day-to-day decisions
* **Shareholders**, who elect the board and vote on major decisions

The board has an ethical role that goes beyond financial oversight. It sets the tone for the entire organization. When a board prioritizes ethics, that priority flows downward. When a board ignores ethical concerns in favor of growth, employees get the message.

In technology companies, governance decisions carry unusual weight because the products affect so many people. A board's decision to rush a product to market, cut a safety team, or delay addressing a known bias in an algorithm is a governance decision with ethical consequences for millions.

## 3.2 Corporate Social Responsibility in Technology

**Corporate social responsibility (CSR)** is the idea that businesses have obligations to society that go beyond making money. CSR encompasses a company's efforts to act ethically, contribute to the community, treat employees fairly, and minimize harm.

### Why CSR Matters for Tech Companies

Technology companies face especially high CSR expectations because their products are so deeply woven into daily life. When a tech company behaves irresponsibly, the consequences can scale to billions of people in a matter of hours. There are several practical reasons why CSR matters in the technology sector.

**Gaining community goodwill.** Companies that invest in their communities, support education, and act as responsible neighbors build trust. That trust translates into customer loyalty, better recruiting, and stronger partnerships. When a company loses community trust, as Facebook did after the Cambridge Analytica scandal in 2018, rebuilding it takes years.

**Creating consistent organizational behavior.** A strong CSR commitment gives employees at every level a framework for making decisions. When the company's values are clear, people do not have to guess what is expected of them. Without that framework, individual employees are left to make ethical judgments on their own, and the results are inconsistent.

**Fostering good business practices.** Companies that take CSR seriously tend to adopt better internal processes. They document their decisions, audit their systems, and set measurable goals. These habits improve not just ethics but overall operational quality.

**Protecting the organization from legal action.** Proactive CSR efforts can reduce legal risk. Companies that address potential harms before they become lawsuits or regulatory actions save money and protect their reputations.

**Avoiding unfavorable publicity.** In the age of social media, a single ethical failure can become a global news story within hours. Companies with strong CSR programs are better positioned to prevent those failures and to respond effectively when problems do arise.

### The Gig Economy and Outsourcing Ethics

CSR becomes more complicated when companies rely on workers who are not technically employees. **Contingent workers**, including gig workers, freelancers, and outsourced contractors, often lack the benefits, protections, and job security that full-time employees receive.

Consider a ride-sharing company that classifies its drivers as independent contractors rather than employees. The drivers do not receive health insurance, paid time off, or workers' compensation. The company benefits from lower labor costs. But do those drivers count as stakeholders? Stakeholder theory says yes. CSR frameworks suggest that a company's ethical obligations extend to everyone who contributes to its success, not just those on the official payroll.

Outsourcing raises similar concerns. When a major technology company contracts with overseas workers to moderate disturbing content at low wages, the company benefits from cheaper labor, but the workers may face psychological harm without adequate support. The question is whether the contracting company bears ethical responsibility for how those workers are treated, even if a third-party staffing agency technically employs them.

### Case Study 3.1 - The Content Moderator Crisis 📋

Apex Staffing argues that it follows local labor laws and that GlobalTech's content volume and speed requirements make it impossible to provide longer review times or additional breaks.

* **Apex Staffing management** who must balance worker welfare against the terms of their contract with GlobalTech
* **Regulators and lawmakers** who are evaluating whether platform companies should be held responsible for the welfare of contracted workers

## 3.3 Building Ethical Organizations

Knowing that CSR matters is one thing. Actually building an organization that behaves ethically is another. This section covers the practical tools companies use to move from good intentions to consistent ethical behavior.

### Appointing a Chief Ethics Officer

Some organizations appoint a **Chief Ethics Officer** or a similar executive-level role responsible for overseeing the company's ethical conduct. This person typically reports directly to the CEO or the board of directors. Their job is to identify ethical risks, develop policies, investigate complaints, and serve as a resource for employees facing ethical dilemmas.

Having a dedicated ethics officer sends a signal throughout the organization: ethics is not an afterthought. It is a leadership priority. However, the role is only effective if the officer has real authority and independence. An ethics officer who can be overruled or fired for raising uncomfortable concerns provides little actual protection.

### Establishing a Code of Ethics

A **code of ethics** is a written document that outlines the ethical standards an organization expects from its employees. A good code of ethics does more than list rules. It describes the values behind those rules and provides guidance for situations the rules do not explicitly cover.

Effective codes of ethics share several features:

* They are written in clear, accessible language that all employees can understand
* They address the specific ethical challenges the organization is most likely to face
* They include examples and scenarios, not just abstract principles
* They describe how to report ethical concerns and what protections exist for people who report them
* They are reviewed and updated regularly as the organization and its industry change

A code of ethics that sits in an employee handbook and is never discussed is just a document. To matter, it must be woven into training, performance reviews, and everyday conversations.

### Conducting Social Audits

A **social audit** is a formal review of a company's impact on its stakeholders and communities. It evaluates how well the company is living up to its stated values and CSR commitments. Social audits can cover environmental impact, labor practices, community engagement, supply chain ethics, and more.

The key benefit of a social audit is accountability. It forces the company to measure its ethical performance against specific criteria, rather than relying on vague claims about being "committed to doing the right thing."

### Ethics Training and Performance Appraisals

Training is how organizations translate ethical policies into employee behavior. Effective ethics training is not a one-time event. It happens regularly, uses realistic scenarios, and gives employees practice making ethical decisions in low-stakes settings so they are better prepared for high-stakes situations.

Some organizations go further by including **ethical criteria in performance appraisals**. This means that employees are evaluated not just on what they achieved but on how they achieved it. When ethical behavior is part of how promotions and raises are determined, employees have a concrete incentive to act with integrity.

## 3.4 AI Ethics: Governance, Accountability, and the Global Landscape

This section is the AI ethics anchor for the entire course. The principles introduced here will reappear in every module that follows. Take time with this material. It forms the foundation for how you will think about AI throughout the rest of CIS111.

### Why AI Ethics Is a Corporate Governance Issue

Artificial intelligence systems make decisions that affect hiring, lending, healthcare, criminal justice, content moderation, and countless other areas. When those systems cause harm, the question of accountability becomes a governance question: who inside the organization approved the system? Who tested it? Who monitored its performance after deployment? And who is responsible when something goes wrong?

AI governance is not just a technical problem. It is a leadership problem. The choices companies make about how to develop, test, deploy, and monitor AI systems reflect the same governance structures you learned about in Section 3.1. A board that demands fast AI deployment without investing in safety testing is making a governance decision. A CEO who appoints an AI ethics team but gives it no real authority is making a governance decision. These decisions have consequences.

### Core AI Ethics Principles

Several principles have emerged as foundations for responsible AI development. You will see these terms throughout the rest of this course.

**Fairness.** AI systems should treat all people equitably. They should not produce outcomes that systematically disadvantage one group over another based on race, gender, age, disability, or other protected characteristics.

**Transparency.** Organizations should be open about how their AI systems work, what data they use, and how decisions are made. Users and affected communities deserve to know when AI is being used and how it affects them.

**Accountability.** There must be clear lines of responsibility for AI systems. When an AI system causes harm, specific people and organizations must be answerable for that harm.

**Explainability.** AI systems should be able to explain their decisions in terms that humans can understand. A hiring tool that rejects a candidate should be able to articulate why, in language the candidate and a human reviewer can evaluate.

**Human oversight.** AI systems should operate under meaningful human supervision. Critical decisions, especially those affecting people's rights, opportunities, or safety, should include human review rather than being fully automated.

### Algorithmic Bias: How It Happens and Who It Harms

**Algorithmic bias** occurs when an AI system produces systematically unfair outcomes. Bias can enter AI systems at multiple points.

**Biased training data.** AI systems learn from historical data. If that data reflects past discrimination, the AI will reproduce and sometimes amplify that discrimination. For example, if a hiring algorithm is trained on a company's past hiring decisions, and those decisions favored men over women, the algorithm will learn to favor men over women.

**Biased design choices.** The people who build AI systems make decisions about what data to use, what outcomes to optimize for, and how to measure success. Those choices reflect the perspectives and blind spots of the development team. A team that lacks diversity may fail to consider how the system could affect people unlike themselves.

**Biased deployment context.** Even a well-designed AI system can produce biased outcomes if it is deployed in a context it was not designed for. A facial recognition system trained primarily on lighter-skinned faces will perform poorly on darker-skinned faces, not because the algorithm is intentionally biased, but because the training data was not representative.

The harm from algorithmic bias falls disproportionately on communities that are already marginalized. People of color, women, people with disabilities, and low-income populations are more likely to be affected by biased AI systems in hiring, lending, healthcare, and criminal justice.

### The Global AI Landscape

Different organizations and different countries approach AI ethics in different ways. Understanding that landscape matters because AI systems cross borders. A model trained in one country may be deployed worldwide, carrying its developers' ethical assumptions with it.

**Corporate approaches vary widely.** Companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, and others have published AI safety principles, but the specifics differ. Some companies prioritize transparency and publish detailed information about their models. Others treat their models as proprietary and share less. Some invest heavily in safety research before releasing products. Others move faster and address problems after deployment.

**National context shapes ethical priorities.** The European Union has taken a precautionary approach with the **EU AI Act**, which categorizes AI systems by risk level and imposes strict requirements on high-risk applications like hiring tools, law enforcement systems, and critical infrastructure. The United States has taken a more sector-specific approach, with the **NIST AI Risk Management Framework** providing voluntary guidelines rather than binding regulations. China has enacted AI regulations that focus heavily on content control and social stability. These differences reflect different cultural values, legal traditions, and political priorities.

**International frameworks are emerging.** The **IEEE Ethically Aligned Design** initiative offers engineering-focused guidelines for AI development. The OECD has published AI principles that many countries have endorsed. But no single global standard exists yet, and the gap between stated principles and actual practice remains wide.

### Who Is Accountable When AI Causes Harm?

This is one of the hardest questions in AI ethics, and it does not have a settled answer. Consider a scenario: a hospital uses an AI diagnostic tool that fails to detect cancer in a patient. The patient's diagnosis is delayed, and the cancer spreads. Who is responsible?

* The company that built the AI tool?
* The hospital that chose to use it?
* The doctor who relied on its recommendation?
* The regulators who approved it (or failed to regulate it)?

The accountability gap in AI is a real and growing problem. When multiple parties are involved in building, deploying, and using an AI system, responsibility can become diffused to the point where nobody is effectively held accountable. This is why clear governance structures matter. Organizations that deploy AI need to define, in advance, who is responsible for monitoring the system, responding to errors, and compensating people who are harmed.

### Green Computing and the Environmental Ethics of AI

AI governance is not just about fairness and accountability. It also involves environmental responsibility. Training large AI models requires enormous amounts of computing power, which consumes electricity and generates carbon emissions. Researchers have estimated that training a single large language model can produce as much carbon dioxide as several cars produce over their entire lifetimes.

**Green computing** refers to the practice of designing, manufacturing, using, and disposing of computing resources in environmentally responsible ways. For AI specifically, green computing raises questions about whether the benefits of ever-larger models justify their environmental costs, and whether companies have an obligation to measure and disclose the carbon footprint of their AI systems.

## 3.6 Summary and Retrieval 💡

### Key Concepts

* **Shareholder theory vs. stakeholder theory** represent two fundamentally different views of corporate responsibility. Shareholder theory says a company's primary obligation is to its owners. Stakeholder theory says a company owes obligations to everyone affected by its operations, including employees, customers, communities, and contractors.

* **Corporate governance** is the system that determines who makes decisions within a company and who holds decision-makers accountable. In technology companies, governance decisions carry unusual ethical weight because the products affect millions or billions of people.

* **Corporate social responsibility (CSR)** means that businesses have obligations to society beyond profit. For tech companies, CSR is especially important because of the scale and speed at which their products can cause harm. Practical reasons for CSR include building goodwill, creating consistent behavior, fostering good practices, reducing legal risk, and avoiding bad publicity.

* **Building ethical organizations** requires practical tools: Chief Ethics Officers, codes of ethics, social audits, ethics training, and ethical criteria in performance reviews. Policies that exist only on paper do not change behavior.

* **AI ethics is a corporate governance issue.** The five core principles are fairness, transparency, accountability, explainability, and human oversight. Algorithmic bias can enter AI systems through biased training data, biased design choices, or biased deployment contexts. The harm falls disproportionately on already-marginalized communities.

* **The global AI landscape** reflects different ethical priorities. The EU AI Act takes a precautionary, risk-tiered approach. The US NIST AI Risk Management Framework provides voluntary guidelines. Corporate AI ethics commitments vary widely in substance and enforcement. No single global standard exists yet.

### Key Terms

**Section 3.1**
* **Shareholder theory:** The view that a corporation's primary obligation is to maximize profits for its owners.
* **Stakeholder theory:** The view that a corporation has obligations to everyone affected by its operations, including employees, customers, communities, and others.
* **Stakeholder:** Any person or group that can affect, or is affected by, a company's actions.
* **Corporate governance:** The system of rules, practices, and processes by which a company is directed and controlled.

**Section 3.2**
* **Corporate social responsibility (CSR):** The idea that businesses have obligations to society that go beyond making money.
* **Contingent workers:** Workers who are not permanent employees, including gig workers, freelancers, and outsourced contractors.
* **Social audit:** A formal review of a company's impact on its stakeholders and communities.

**Section 3.3**
* **Chief Ethics Officer:** An executive-level role responsible for overseeing a company's ethical conduct.
* **Code of ethics:** A written document outlining the ethical standards an organization expects from its employees.

**Section 3.4**
* **Fairness (AI):** The principle that AI systems should treat all people equitably and not systematically disadvantage any group.
* **Transparency (AI):** The principle that organizations should be open about how their AI systems work and how decisions are made.
* **Accountability (AI):** The principle that clear lines of responsibility must exist for AI systems, so specific people or organizations are answerable when harm occurs.
* **Explainability (AI):** The principle that AI systems should be able to explain their decisions in terms humans can understand.
* **Human oversight (AI):** The principle that AI systems should operate under meaningful human supervision, especially for critical decisions.
* **Algorithmic bias:** Systematically unfair outcomes produced by an AI system, often resulting from biased training data, biased design choices, or biased deployment contexts.
* **EU AI Act:** European Union legislation that categorizes AI systems by risk level and imposes requirements on high-risk applications.

## Further Reading 📖

* **Markkula Center for Applied Ethics: "Ethics in Technology Practice."** A collection of case studies and toolkits for applying ethical reasoning to technology decisions. Free online resource from Santa Clara University.

* **NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0).** The National Institute of Standards and Technology's voluntary framework for managing AI risks. Free to download at nist.gov. This document provides practical guidance for organizations developing or deploying AI systems.
