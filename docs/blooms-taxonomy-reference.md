# Bloom's Taxonomy Reference for CIS111

This guide helps you write effective learning objectives using Revised Bloom's Taxonomy (RBT), adapted for an ethics-focused IT course.

## Overview

**Bloom's Taxonomy** categorizes learning objectives into six cognitive levels, from simple recall to complex creation. Each level builds on the previous one.

For **CIS111 (introductory IT ethics)**, the full range of cognitive levels is appropriate because ethical reasoning requires higher-order thinking even in introductory courses. However, early modules should emphasize Remember, Understand, and Apply, while later modules progress to Analyze, Evaluate, and Create.

### The Six Cognitive Levels

```
CREATE          ▲
EVALUATE        │ Increasing
ANALYZE         │ Complexity
APPLY           │
UNDERSTAND      │
REMEMBER        │
                ▼
```

---

## Level 1: Remember (Knowledge Recall)

**Definition:** Retrieve relevant knowledge from memory. Recognize or recall facts, terms, concepts, or procedures.

**When to use in CIS111:** Foundational knowledge of ethical theories, legal terms, and key concepts.

**Action Verbs:**
* list, recall, define, identify, label, recognize, name, state, describe (basic facts), locate, match

### Examples for CIS111

**Good:**
* **Define** the key principles of utilitarianism, deontology, and virtue ethics
* **Identify** the main provisions of HIPAA relevant to IT professionals
* **List** the core elements of the ACM Code of Ethics
* **Recall** the legal protections available to whistleblowers under Sarbanes-Oxley

---

## Level 2: Understand (Comprehension)

**Definition:** Construct meaning from instructional messages. Explain ideas or concepts in your own words.

**When to use in CIS111:** Ensuring students grasp ethical concepts and can explain them clearly.

**Action Verbs:**
* explain, describe, summarize, paraphrase, contrast, interpret, classify, compare, illustrate, categorize, discuss, distinguish, infer

### Examples for CIS111

**Good:**
* **Explain** how stakeholder theory differs from shareholder theory in corporate governance
* **Describe** the ethical tensions between employee privacy and employer monitoring
* **Summarize** the fair use doctrine and its limitations in digital contexts
* **Interpret** how GDPR requirements affect IT decision-making in global organizations

---

## Level 3: Apply (Use in New Situations)

**Definition:** Use knowledge in new situations. Execute or implement procedures in given scenarios.

**When to use in CIS111:** Students applying ethical frameworks to real-world IT scenarios.

**Action Verbs:**
* implement, calculate, solve, demonstrate, use, perform, execute, apply, construct (basic), produce, operate, modify

### Examples for CIS111

**Good:**
* **Apply** utilitarian analysis to determine the most ethical course of action in a data breach scenario
* **Demonstrate** cost/benefit analysis in an IT ethical decision-making context
* **Use** an ethical decision-making framework to evaluate a workplace surveillance policy
* **Apply** copyright and fair use principles to evaluate a software licensing dispute

---

## Level 4: Analyze (Break Down and Examine)

**Definition:** Break material into parts and determine how parts relate. Distinguish between facts and inferences.

**When to use in CIS111:** Examining ethical dilemmas from multiple perspectives, identifying stakeholders and conflicts.

**Action Verbs:**
* differentiate, distinguish, categorize, compare, organize, diagram, analyze, deconstruct, examine, relate, contrast, investigate

### Examples for CIS111

**Good:**
* **Analyze** the competing stakeholder interests in a corporate data privacy breach
* **Compare** how utilitarian and deontological frameworks lead to different conclusions about employee monitoring
* **Examine** the ethical implications of algorithmic bias in hiring software
* **Differentiate** between ethical, legal, and practical considerations in a whistleblowing scenario

---

## Level 5: Evaluate (Make Judgments)

**Definition:** Make judgments based on criteria and standards. Critique methods or results.

**When to use in CIS111:** Judging ethical positions, critiquing policies, defending ethical arguments. **More appropriate in CIS111 than in CIS215** because ethics requires value judgments.

**Action Verbs:**
* critique, judge, justify, argue, defend, evaluate, assess, prioritize, rate, recommend, validate

### Examples for CIS111

**Good:**
* **Evaluate** whether a company's response to a data breach was ethically adequate
* **Critique** the effectiveness of the ACM Code of Ethics in addressing AI-related challenges
* **Justify** a position on whether social media platforms should be liable for user-generated hate speech
* **Assess** the ethical adequacy of current government regulations on facial recognition technology

---

## Level 6: Create (Produce New Work)

**Definition:** Put elements together to form a novel, coherent whole. Reorganize elements into a new pattern.

**When to use in CIS111:** Formulating policies, developing ethical frameworks, designing solutions. Used primarily in later modules and capstone work.

**Action Verbs:**
* design, formulate, build, compose, develop, generate, construct, plan, produce, create, devise

### Examples for CIS111

**Good:**
* **Formulate** a security policy for a small business that balances employee privacy with organizational security needs
* **Develop** an ethical framework for evaluating emerging AI technologies in healthcare
* **Design** a whistleblower protection program for a technology company
* **Construct** an argument for or against government regulation of social media content moderation

---

## Recommended Distribution for CIS111

### Part I - Modules 1-3 (Foundations)
* 1-2 objectives at Remember/Understand level
* 1 objective at Apply level
* **Focus:** Building vocabulary and conceptual understanding

### Part II - Modules 4-6 (Digital Rights)
* 1 objective at Understand level
* 1-2 objectives at Apply/Analyze level
* **Focus:** Applying frameworks to specific domains

### Part III - Modules 7-9 (Legal Frameworks)
* 1 objective at Apply level
* 1-2 objectives at Analyze/Evaluate level
* **Focus:** Critical analysis of laws and regulations

### Part IV - Modules 10-12 (Security & Professional Practice)
* 1 objective at Analyze/Evaluate level
* 1-2 objectives at Evaluate/Create level
* **Focus:** Synthesis, judgment, and policy formulation

---

## Key Difference from CIS215

In **CIS215** (data analytics), most objectives stay at Remember, Understand, and Apply because the focus is on procedural/technical skills.

In **CIS111** (ethics), the full range of Bloom's levels is used more evenly because:
* Ethics requires **evaluation** and **judgment** even at introductory levels
* Students must **analyze** competing perspectives, not just follow procedures
* Capstone work requires **creating** original ethical arguments and policies
* The course is about **thinking critically**, not executing technical tasks

---

## Quality Checklist for Learning Objectives

Before finalizing module learning objectives, verify:

* [ ] 2-3 objectives per module
* [ ] Each objective starts with ONE Bloom's action verb (bolded)
* [ ] Each objective references the CLO(s) it addresses
* [ ] Objectives progress from simpler to more complex across the course
* [ ] Objectives are specific and measurable
* [ ] Objectives can be assessed through discussion, analysis, or project work
* [ ] No vague verbs (understand, know, learn, appreciate)
* [ ] Format: "By the end of this module, you will be able to:"

---

## Quick Reference: Verb Selection for Ethics

**Need foundational knowledge?** Remember verbs (define, identify, list)

**Need conceptual understanding?** Understand verbs (explain, describe, summarize)

**Need practical application?** Apply verbs (apply, demonstrate, use)

**Need critical analysis?** Analyze verbs (analyze, compare, examine)

**Need ethical judgment?** Evaluate verbs (evaluate, critique, justify, assess)

**Need policy/framework creation?** Create verbs (formulate, develop, design, construct)
